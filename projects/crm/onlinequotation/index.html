From 14 Days to 1 Hour: Building a Quotation System That Scales

Reduced quote turnaround by 96%, unlocked $5M in revenue, and scaled quoting to 200+ installers without adding headcount.

What this project demonstrates
  • Strategic build-vs-buy decision making under revenue pressure
  • Designing systems for non-technical users at scale
  • Low-code + backend reuse to ship 10× faster
  • Operational automation as a growth lever, not just efficiency
  • Change management across 200+ service providers
The challenge

A rapidly growing Southeast Asian home services marketplace (3× YoY growth) was being slowed by a paper-based quotation process that took 7–14 days. Customers routinely booked with faster competitors, and 50% of customer complaints were tied directly to quote wait times.

The key constraint: over 90% of 200+ installers had never used digital tools. Any solution had to work immediately for non-technical users.

The approach

1. Diagnose before building
Applied Lean Six Sigma principles and interviewed 30+ installers to map the full quotation flow. The bottleneck wasn’t effort—it was the absence of a system designed for on-site reality.

2. Make a deliberate build-vs-buy trade-off
A custom build was estimated at six months—too slow under revenue pressure. I repurposed the internal pricing engine and layered a mobile-friendly, low-code interface on top. MVP shipped in 3 weeks.

3. Optimize for adoption, not elegance
Delivered a PWA installers could pin to their home screen, embedded conditional pricing logic, and added human review checkpoints to balance automation with trust.

Key learnings: The blueprint for scalable systems
Speed beats perfection under revenue pressure Shipping a constrained, “good enough” system quickly created immediate business impact.
Adoption matters more than technical elegance Designing for non-technical users and investing in enablement was as critical as the system itself.
Build-vs-buy is a strategic decision Leveraging low-code platforms and existing infrastructure eliminated months of engineering work.
Technical debt can be intentional When documented and planned, trade-offs can accelerate growth without sacrificing long-term stability.
